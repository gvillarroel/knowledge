## BioC passage 0002

Locator metadata: offset=62; section_type=ABSTRACT; passage_type=abstract; text_sha256=9b7238fd9f6adfd5a100d3befbd72d4aa76aff55b93505e74a0109081a7672df

Ultraviolet (UV) filters are used widely in cosmetics, plastics, adhesives and other industrial products to protect human skin or products against direct exposure to deleterious UV radiation. With growing usage and mis-disposition of UV filters, they currently represent a new class of contaminants of emerging concern with increasingly reported adverse effects to humans and other organisms. Exposure to UV filters induce various endocrine disrupting effects, as revealed by increasing number of toxicological studies performed in recent years. It is necessary to compile a systematic review on the current research status on endocrine disrupting effects of UV filters toward different organisms. We therefore summarized the recent advances on the evaluation of the potential endocrine disruptors and the mechanism of toxicity for many kinds of UV filters such as benzophenones, camphor derivatives and cinnamate derivatives.

## BioC passage 0004

Locator metadata: offset=1005; section_type=INTRO; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=308be1909084cc9505199f8069142ce7ab77f9369002bf208364e6dd29daf68c

Ultraviolet (UV) filters are a class of chemicals that can absorb or reflect UV light in the ultraviolet A (UVA) range and ultraviolet B (UVB) range with specific wavelengths between 320 and 400 nm, 290 and 320 nm, respectively. They can protect human skin against direct exposure to deleterious UV radiation. Many kinds of organic UV filters were incorporated into cosmetics, plastics, adhesives and other industrial products to avoid potential UV-induced damage. There are 16 UV filters permitted to be used in cosmetics by the US Food and Drug Administration and 27 components permitted to be used in cosmetics by the EU Scientific Committee on Consumer Products.

## BioC passage 0005

Locator metadata: offset=1672; section_type=INTRO; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=39a9c0957f03bc95f3861d8b72a6b0f16ce662073ca147b74860a97b16845ed7

Many UV filters were produced in large quantities and used widely. Residues of UV filters have been detected in multiple environmental matrices including wastewater treatment plants, surface water, sewage sludge, river sediments, fish, human milk and placenta. UV filters can be bioaccumulated in organisms due to their persistence, stability and lipophilicity. They are now becoming contaminants of emerging concern. UV filters were reported to induce acute toxicities, developmental toxicities and reproductive toxicities to different organisms.

2.1. Benzophenones

## BioC passage 0009

Locator metadata: offset=3010; section_type=INTRO; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=36dd021542cbde194f5573848871aea539f17f4515cf9fb1ccaaf925209c2da3

Benzophenone (BP)-type UV filters were used widely in many cosmetics for the protection of skin from UVA and UVB light. Their molecular structures have a diarylketone scaffold with different substitute groups. Residues of BPs were detected in wastewater, surface water, soil, sediment, human urine and breast milk. Many BPs were identified as endocrine disruptors and were involved in the disruption of the hypothalamic–pituitary–gonadal system. As revealed by various in vivo, in vitro bioassays and in silico methods, BPs showed multiple endocrine disrupting effects toward estrogen receptor (ER), androgen receptor (AR), progesterone receptor (PR) and other nuclear receptors.

2.1.1. Estrogenic Disrupting Effects

## BioC passage 0011

Locator metadata: offset=3731; section_type=INTRO; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=dc9fd08503560be80bb60949f28be35f761db71a424fd5961184f1887640cd57

BP-type UV filters could cause multiple estrogenic effects, developmental and reproductive toxicity as revealed by cell-based bioassay. BP-3 was reported to cause a dose-dependent increase of uterine weight of immature Long-Evans rats by the activation of ERα and ERβ. BP-2 caused estrogenic effects such as vitellogenin (VTG) induction in fish. BPs such as BP-1, BP-2, BP-3 and BP-4 caused estrogenic activity, developmental and reproductive toxicity to fish and rat. BPs showed moderate potency to activate the proliferation of MCF-7 breast cancer cell and estrogen-responsive CHO cells at concentrations of the order of micromolar and lower. This potency ranks as 4-hydroxy-benzophenone > 4,4′-dihydroxy-benzophenone > BP-8 > 2,3,4,4′-tetrahydroxy-benzophenone > BP-2 > 2,4, 4′-trihydroxy-benzophenone. However, there was no significant induction of proliferation induced by BP, BP-1, BP-3 and 2,3,4-trihydroxy-benzophenone. Molecular modeling can provide atomic-level information and has been well used to probe interactions of chemicals with biomacromolecules. BPs interacted with residues Glu353, Arg394 or Phe404 of ERα ligand binding domain and such binding mode enhanced binding stability, contributing partly to their estrogenic activities.

Biotransformation or chemical transformation of BPs may have influence on their endocrine disrupting effects. BP-3 can be metabolized within human body and can be metabolized to various metabolites including BP-1 and BP-3. BP-1 was detected in human urine and BP-1 and BP-8 were detected in rats. 2.1.2. Androgenic Disrupting Effects

BP-1, BP-2 and BP-3 showed no agonistic activity toward AR; however, they exhibited anti-androgenic activity in various cells-based bioassays. Camphor derivatives are highly effective UVB-absorbers incorporated in many kinds of cosmetics. These chemicals have high bioconcentration factors and can be bioaccumulated in tissues of organisms after prolonged exposure. The camphor derivatives such as 4-methylbenzylidene camphor (4-MBC) and 3-benzylidene camphor (3-BC) are very lipophilic and can be easily absorbed after direct contact with the skin. 4-MBC and 3-BC were revealed as potential endocrine disruptors, adversely affecting the reproduction and development of many organisms.

2.3. Cinnamate Derivatives

## BioC passage 0026

Locator metadata: offset=10435; section_type=INTRO; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=22f42af7f49b3726f7a4e755587d877c9ea5b010c3cd733f51758a38e2da1c12

Cinnamate derivatives are the most frequently used cosmetic UV filters with the high efficiency to absorb UVA or UVB light. Their molecular structures have a special unsaturated bond between the aromatic ring and the carboxyl group, allowing the molecule to better absorb the 305 nm wavelength UV. Their residues were detected in wastewater, surface water, sewage sludge, fish and marine mammals. Octyl methoxycinnamate (OMC) is one of the most commonly-used UV filters. It was listed as one of 27 UV filters approved for use in cosmetics formulations in the EU and US. OMC has disrupting activities toward ER, AR, PR and THR as reported by multiple in vitro and in vivo studies.

3. Perspectives

## BioC passage 0034

Locator metadata: offset=13364; section_type=DISCUSS; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=e239d589de2057c8fd8d067d17d6ab396a23702cc5840577715b9bd35fbd4f86

With the continuous demand of cosmetics, plastics and various industrial products containing UV filters, the production and application of UV filters, especially new type of UV filters will be increased. For better risk assessment of these chemicals and their metabolites, the investigation of endocrine disrupting effects caused by direct and indirect exposure to UV filters is currently becoming a research hotspot. Adverse outcome pathways of emerging UV filters should be well characterized for their potential disruption of the hypothalamus-pituitary-gonadal endocrine axis. Although multiple in vivo and in vitro studies have investigated the adverse effects of UV filters, the underlying mechanism of endocrine disruption should be further explored at the atomic level. Considering the versatile role of computational toxicology for the study of physiochemical properties of organic contaminants and their interactions with various biomacromolecules, more in silico studies should be performed, primarily for UV filters to probe the molecular initiating event toward target receptors.
