## BioC passage 0013

Locator metadata: offset=1634; section_type=INTRO; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=50d2ebb79ce2d98e68cb493eecde127cb0a1e4971275cddf044bfc159733d741

Recent studies highlight a pressing racial/ethnic disparity in hair and personal care product use, with Black women generally using more hair oils, lotions, chemical relaxers, leave-in conditioners, and placenta-containing products. Of importance, data suggest that these products may contain hormonally active ingredients, such as endocrine disrupting chemicals (EDCs), including lower molecular weight phthalates and parabens. Interestingly, nationally representative data from the United States shows that Black women have among the highest concentrations of these personal care product chemicals compared to other racial/ethnic groups, with hair products potentially being an important source of hormonally active chemical exposure. Chemicals contained in hair products have been linked to hormonally mediated diseases that are more prevalent among Black women and girls, including early puberty, preterm birth, obesity, and diabetes. As such, determining whether ingredients in hair products could contribute to higher exposure to hormonally active chemicals and related diseases among Black women is of key importance to reducing these health disparities.

## BioC passage 0014

Locator metadata: offset=2796; section_type=INTRO; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=76aadb25b1067fc1ff1464f61dd09d563793614fb25a18ab9c434984b5751745

Understanding the drivers of hair product use as a link to higher hormonally active chemical exposures and hormone-mediated disease risk provides critical insight into why these disparities may exist. An important driver of hair product use among Black women is Eurocentric beauty standards that have impacted social and economic position from post-slavery and colonization to modern day. Specifically, straighter hair represented ideal beauty standards and higher social status, requiring Black women to assimilate to white culture. A number of Black pioneers developed hair maintenance systems that provided and maintained straighter hair, including Anna Turbo Malone, Elroy J. Duncan, and Madame C.J. Walker. Beyond rinse-out soaps to clean hair, these products included leave-in hair oils and stimulating lotions. Today, Black hair care is a half-trillion dollar industry, with Black women spending more money on hair styling products than any other ethnic group. That said, even recent legislation in the United States has had to be enacted to ensure Black women can wear their hair in ways that could allow for less chemical exposure. Of concern, the use of these products may be placing Black women at increased exposure to toxic chemicals—including EDCs—due to these underlying social and political factors that are systemic drivers of beauty and economic status.

## BioC passage 0015

Locator metadata: offset=4172; section_type=INTRO; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=11187e3500a4c3e918430fe9454f62af13bbebebb43f1a95ef20c57c9d766928

While some studies have documented individual chemicals and chemical classes contained in hair products, few have evaluated the impact of these products—as chemical mixtures—on alterations to hormone systems. In a study by Myers et al., a number of hair and skin care products were assessed for hormone activity. Of the eight products assessed, only one product was a leave-in hair product, which showed estrogen activity. With leave-in hair products being commonly used by Black women, evaluating whether chemicals in these hair products could be agonist or antagonist of hormone receptors linked to diseases is key. Many of the EDCs identified in hair products are thought to bind to and alter hormone receptors including estrogen, progesterone, androgen, and glucocorticoid receptors. Of note, these specific hormone receptors are involved in numerous biological processes, which if altered, could contribute to the risk of diseases such as diabetes, cardiovascular disease, as well as adverse reproductive health outcomes. Thus, hair product use could contribute to a mixture of chemical exposures that could perturb hormonal processes and affect disease risk.

## BioC passage 0016

Locator metadata: offset=5341; section_type=INTRO; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=7b8aa561fa1a5472a665cbf3b5f7cef0baf19a84370eb83053cdf6951f1ee356

While few studies have evaluated hair products as mixtures, a number of studies have evaluated the hormonal effects of individual chemicals present in hair products. For example, previous work has identified Black hair products as containing phthalates, parabens, alkylphenols, cyclosiloxanes, ethanolamines, and UV filters. Alkylphenols have estrogenic properties, with the ability to alter reproductive parameters in rodents. Similarly, cyclosiloxanes and certain phenols, including certain parabens and UV filters are also estrogenic. Parabens have anti-androgenic properties and can disrupt the glucocorticoid receptor, with implications for glucose and other metabolic pathways. In addition, phthalates also have anti-androgenic properties and have been shown to be peroxisome-proliferator alpha and gamma agonists, impacting lipid and glucose metabolism. Together, these chemicals may operate in complex ways to perturb hormonal pathways.

## BioC passage 0017

Locator metadata: offset=6286; section_type=INTRO; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=966f5c5958f2bc9446fd2acfb81153e566822a5e07e8faca747a01c14abc2e9b

Understanding whether commonly used Black hair products, as chemical mixtures, could bind to and alter hormone signaling will provide critical information to environmental exposure and associated hormone mediated disease disparities. Thus, the objective of this study was to evaluate the extent to which hair products, as EDC mixtures, could bind to and operate as agonists or antagonists of four hormone receptors: estrogen, androgen, progesterone, and glucocorticoid. We selected commonly used hair products based on data from the Greater New York Hair Products Study, which were previously shown to contain EDCs and have been linked to adverse health outcomes. As previous studies found estrogenic chemicals, and estrogenic activity, we hypothesized that these products would be able to bind to the estrogen receptor and act as agonists and included other hormone receptor analyses as exploratory analyses.

## BioC passage 0041

Locator metadata: offset=16251; section_type=DISCUSS; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=a96d6d01252b1abab3754300c855414f6ccfc22472ed5938fb38e7dd394b500f

In this study of six leave-in hair products commonly used by Black women participating in the Greater New York Hair Products Study, we found that all products elicited hormonal activity. In general, the type of hormone activity varied by product, with some displaying agonistic properties and others displaying both partial agonistic and antagonistic properties related to one or more of the hormone receptors. One of the leave-in conditioners (sample 3) and one of the hair oils (sample 4), displayed hormonal activity on all 4 hormone assays, potentially indicating that use of these products may be of particular concern in relation to hormonally-mediated disease. The hair lotion and root stimulator both displayed estrogenic activity, but did not show any androgenic activity, while all other samples acted as androgen antagonists. These findings suggest that products commonly used by Black women may have hormonal activity when each product is considered as a chemical mixture. To our knowledge this is one of the first studies to evaluate the hormonal activity of multiple commonly used Black leave-in hair products.

## BioC passage 0042

Locator metadata: offset=17376; section_type=DISCUSS; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=fb62a96b16b63d3f2ac5fa25bfacd425f14b5b14b8274b5a63b664083705b8a8

Few studies have assessed chemical content of Black hair products. One previous study by Helm et al. using a greater number of hair products from the same study population found that all of the hair products tested contained endocrine disrupting and asthma-associated chemicals. Specifically, the study found that these commonly used hair products contained 45 EDCs, including cyclosiloxanes, parabens, fragrance marker diethyl phthalate (DEP), and nonylphenols. In an earlier study in the same population, 50% of Black women reported using products containing hormones or placenta compared to only 7% of white women based on product label information. A separate study focusing more broadly on personal care products, found that a number of products, including the only tested hair product, displayed hormonal activity using a similar reporter gene assay model. Our study suggests the need to further evaluate hormonal activity in personal care products, particularly those used by individuals with higher use and a higher risk of many hormonally mediated adverse health outcomes.

## BioC passage 0043

Locator metadata: offset=18458; section_type=DISCUSS; passage_type=paragraph; text_sha256=57f9be79812d0b19bc2a2304bf868bccb51e57ce058a8b8a66cce23c624bf0b1

Studies show that hair products are very commonly used by Black women, with reported usage of almost 100% for hair oils, >70% for hair lotions, and >50% for leave-in conditioners. These products are often applied to the hair and scalp and not washed out for days or weeks, allowing chemicals in the product to accumulate on the scalp and potentially enter the body transdermally through porous scalp tissue.
